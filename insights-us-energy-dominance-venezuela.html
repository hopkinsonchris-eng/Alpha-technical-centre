The Calorie Trade:
US Energy Dominance
and the Venezuela Reset

The One Big Beautiful Bill has rewritten the rules of US energy production on federal lands and waters. Washington's intervention in Venezuela has rewritten the geopolitics of Latin American oil. Taken together, they represent the most significant restructuring of Western Hemisphere energy since the shale revolution. Here is what it means for asset owners operating in the region.

Alpha Technical Centre Editorial
Houston, Texas · alphalatinamerica.com

Why "Energy Dominance" Is More Than a Slogan

The phrase has always had a political ring to it, but in 2026 it carries specific legislative weight. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed into law on Independence Day 2025, is the most comprehensive restructuring of US energy production rules in a generation. Strip away the political framing and the substance is straightforward: the federal government has made it significantly cheaper, faster, and more predictable to drill on public lands and offshore waters.

The headline provisions matter. Royalty rates on new federal onshore leases have been cut from 16.67% to 12.5% — reversing the Inflation Reduction Act's uplift and returning to the pre-2022 baseline. Offshore royalties have been trimmed similarly. Quarterly onshore lease sales are now mandated across nine western states. The Bureau of Ocean Energy Management must hold at least two Gulf of Mexico lease sales per year through 2039. Alaska leasing has been reopened after years of dormancy, with six Cook Inlet offshore sales scheduled through 2032.

For independent producers, the most commercially significant change may be the restoration of full immediate expensing of intangible drilling costs (IDCs) for companies subject to the corporate alternative minimum tax. IDC expensing — in place since 1913 — allows operators to immediately write off drilling-related costs such as wages, fuel, and site preparation. The IRA had clawed this back under CAMT rules; the OBBBA reverses it. The practical effect is a meaningful reduction in the after-tax cost of drilling new wells, particularly for mid-sized independents who had been operating at a disadvantage relative to the majors.

Permitting timelines have also been addressed, if modestly. Operators can now pay a fee — 125% of agency costs — to secure an environmental assessment within 180 days or a full environmental impact statement within one year. This does not deregulate the process, but it converts timeline uncertainty into a predictable, priceable variable. For project economics, that is worth more than it sounds.

The methane fee, introduced in the IRA and deeply unpopular with producers, has effectively been suspended until 2035. The 45Q carbon capture tax credit has been retained and expanded to include parity for enhanced oil recovery operations — a provision that rewards operators who are already pursuing EOR on mature fields.

"The OBBBA does not promise a production boom — it lowers the floor. The barrels that could not be justified at $65 with 16.67% royalties may work at 12.5%."

What this does not do is guarantee a supply surge. US operators have spent four years building the discipline of capital returns over production growth, and a royalty cut does not override that instinct overnight. US crude production already exceeds 13.6 million barrels per day — close to the structural ceiling given current drilling technology and inventory. The OBBBA is better understood as raising the floor of what is economic than raising the ceiling of what is possible.

Venezuela: The 303-Billion-Barrel Question

On 3 January 2026, US special forces arrested Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro in Caracas. In the days that followed, the Trump administration declared its intention to control the flow of Venezuelan oil through authorised channels — and announced that US companies would invest to rebuild the country's collapsed production infrastructure.

The context matters enormously. Venezuela holds 303 billion barrels of proven reserves — the world's largest — but has been producing less than 860,000 barrels per day, a fraction of its mid-2000s peak above 3 million bpd. Decades of mismanagement under Chávez, accelerated collapse under Maduro, and the compounding effects of US sanctions have left PDVSA as a shell of the operator it once was. Thousands of engineers have left. Infrastructure is severely corroded. Rystad Energy estimates restoring production to 2 million bpd would require approximately $110 billion in capital investment. Restoring it to pre-1999 levels — roughly 3.5 million bpd — would require $100–250 billion and a decade of sustained commitment.

What the market said: When news of the Maduro arrest broke, Brent fell to approximately $60/barrel and WTI dropped below $58. The market's verdict was clear — Venezuela's near-term production upside is limited, and the world is already well-supplied. The strategic significance of the intervention lies elsewhere.

The strategic significance is threefold. First, it removes Venezuela as a supplier to China, Russia, and Iran — all of whom had established oil-for-loan arrangements with Maduro's government. US control over Venezuelan oil flows becomes a direct instrument of competition with Beijing, which had been importing 30–50 million barrels through PDVSA joint ventures. Second, it establishes a precedent — as Chilean President Boric noted immediately — that the US is prepared to use military force to advance energy-related national security objectives in the Western Hemisphere. Third, it creates a potential — if long-dated — source of incremental supply that Washington would like to see arrive at $50 oil rather than $80.

This last point is the "calorie trade." The administration's stated goal of driving WTI to $50 per barrel is essentially an inflation strategy: cheaper energy is a calorie in the economic diet of every American household and every US manufacturer. Venezuelan barrels — even a partial recovery to 1.5 million bpd — add to the global supply picture in ways that support that price objective over a 3–5 year horizon, particularly if combined with continued US production discipline at current levels.

"Venezuela's barrels are not a near-term supply story. They are a long-dated option on the US administration's geopolitical patience — and the investment appetite of the majors who were burned there before."

The operational reality is more sobering. The majors that Trump hopes will rush back to Venezuela have been expropriated there — some twice. ConocoPhillips, ExxonMobil, and others spent years in international arbitration recovering losses from earlier Venezuelan nationalisations. No senior executive of a major oil company is going to commit billions of dollars to Venezuelan field rehabilitation without contractual frameworks that simply do not yet exist. The regulatory pathway, governance structure, and terms of any new operating agreements remain entirely undefined. The geopolitical intent is clear; the commercial mechanism is not.

What This Reshapes for the Rest of Latin America

The combination of the OBBBA and the Venezuela intervention creates a specific new environment for upstream operators across the region — and it is not uniformly negative.

For Brazil and Argentina, the strategic picture improves. Both countries benefit from the displacement of Chinese investment capital from Venezuela. Chinese operators who had positioned in Venezuelan joint ventures — most notably through PDVSA arrangements — are being pushed out of a market that accounted for a meaningful share of their Latin American upstream exposure. That capital does not disappear; it looks for a home. Brazil's transparent regulatory framework and Argentina's RIGI investment incentives make both countries natural destinations.

For Guyana, US geopolitical alignment is structural capital. ExxonMobil's dominance of the Stabroek Block was already attractive to the administration on energy security grounds. The US intervention in Venezuela — explicitly framed by the National Security Strategy as denying non-hemispheric competitors access to Western Hemisphere resources — reinforces the logic of the Exxon-Guyana relationship as a strategic asset, not merely a commercial one.

For independent operators and technical advisors working across the region, the most immediate implication is a change in the risk calculus around political environment. Countries that sit within the US sphere of geopolitical alignment — which now includes a broader and more actively enforced definition of "Western Hemisphere" — benefit from implicit security backstopping that was not priced in five years ago. Countries that maintained closer ties to Chinese or Russian energy capital now carry elevated sovereign risk in the eyes of US-aligned capital providers.

This is the environment in which Alpha Technical Centre operates. We do not offer geopolitical analysis as a service — we offer reservoir engineering, production modelling, and economic frameworks. But the quality of our technical work ultimately lands in a geopolitical context, and understanding that context is part of doing the job well.

The Honest Caveats

Clarity requires acknowledging what is genuinely uncertain. The OBBBA's production uplift is real but modest in the near term — lower royalties do not override $60 oil. The Venezuela intervention has created legal, diplomatic, and commercial ambiguity that could persist for years, and there is no guarantee that the oil majors will re-engage on terms that work. The methane regulatory environment — suspended federally but tightening in several US states and across the EU — creates compliance complexity for operators with global portfolios.

More fundamentally, the price environment is the constraint that no policy can fully override. If WTI stays below $60, the OBBBA provisions help at the margin but do not transform project economics. The administration's desire for $50 oil — if achieved — helps US consumers and hurts nearly every producer in this analysis, including Venezuelan rehabilitation, Brazilian pre-salt at its thinner margins, and Argentine Vaca Muerta on its higher-cost wells. There is a tension at the heart of the energy dominance agenda between wanting more production and wanting lower prices, and it has not been resolved.

What is clear is that the rules have changed, the geopolitical map of the region has shifted, and the window for thoughtful, well-capitalised, technically rigorous upstream engagement in Latin America is open. Whether it stays open long enough for the majors to return to Venezuela, for Argentina to build its LNG export infrastructure, and for Guyana to develop the institutional capacity its oil wealth requires — those are questions that 2027 and 2028 will answer.
